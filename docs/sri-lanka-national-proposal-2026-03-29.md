## Part I: The Problem Sri Lanka Faces

### 1.1 The Architecture of Collapse

The 2022 crisis was not caused by Sri Lanka's people. It was caused by the architecture they were governed by.

Successive governments borrowed in foreign currencies against earnings in local ones. They denominated sovereign debt to international creditors who had no stake in whether the borrowing succeeded. They built an economy dependent on tourism and remittances — sectors with no domestic capital accumulation logic — while making infrastructure decisions through unaccountable channels that generated neither efficiency nor equity.

When the global environment shifted, there were no shock absorbers. Foreign reserves depleted to near zero. The rupee lost more than half its value. A country of 22 million people found itself unable to import fuel, medicine, or food at volumes sufficient to function. Power cuts of ten hours per day. Operating rooms that went dark. Queues of kilometers.

The crisis did not occur because Sri Lanka lacked intelligent, capable people. It occurred because the systems those people were given were designed for extraction, not for creation.

### 1.2 The Recovery Gap

The IMF program has stabilized the macroeconomic environment. Reserves have recovered to $6.9 billion. Inflation has dropped to 2.4%. GDP growth reached 5.4% in Q3 2025. The debt restructuring, painful as it was, created the conditions for operational continuity.

But stabilization is not transformation. The underlying architecture that produced the collapse has not been replaced — it has been patched. Sri Lanka remains exposed to the same structural vulnerabilities:

- **Dependency on foreign debt:** The country continues to service restructured debt denominated in foreign currencies, denominated against an economy whose primary earners (remittances, tourism) have limited scaling capacity.

- **Employment structure:** Youth unemployment and the skills mismatch between educational output and economic demand continue to drive brain drain. The diaspora — Sri Lanka's most internationally mobile, highest-skilled citizens — leave precisely when the country needs them most.

- **Governance credibility deficit:** The crisis produced deep institutional distrust. The Dissanayake government was elected explicitly on a clean governance mandate. Meeting that mandate requires not just political intent but technical infrastructure that makes governance decisions verifiable and auditable.

- **Digital infrastructure gap:** Sri Lanka has committed $98 million to digital infrastructure in 2026. The SL-UDI blockchain identity initiative is operational. The National Digital Economy Strategy 2030 is published. But the country lacks a technology partner with the architecture to execute on these commitments at scale.

### 1.3 The Window

There is a rare alignment present in Sri Lanka in March 2026 that will not persist indefinitely:

- A president who personally chairs the Ministry of Digital Economy
- A regulatory framework at Port City Colombo that permits crypto and digital asset operations today
- A green bond framework ($11.26 billion target) activated by UNDP that requires qualifying projects
- 1.16 million cryptocurrency users — 4.97% population penetration, among the highest in South Asia — demonstrating that the population has already self-organized around alternative financial systems
- A political mandate for clean governance that is technically compatible with on-chain transparency

Each of these conditions exists independently. The question is whether anyone assembles them into a coherent architecture before the window moves.

We intend to be the ones who do.

The economic model is not speculative. It is operational:

- Contributors enter the Loop through verified work. Design, code, data processing, physical labor, education delivery, community operations — all are contribution types that generate a DPC (Dynamic Proximity Calculus) score.

## Part VI: Governance Architecture

### 6.1 The Design Challenge

MY3YE is a decentralized, community-governed movement. Sri Lanka is a sovereign state with parliamentary democracy, IMF oversight, and constitutional authority. The governance model must respect both without subordinating either.

This is a solvable problem. The solution is a three-layer architecture that cleanly delineates what each party controls.

### 6.2 Three-Layer Structure

**Layer 1 — Operational Governance (MY3YE internal)**

MY3YE's internal governance — DPC scoring, contributor elections, Loop economics, community standards, technology architecture decisions — is governed entirely by the MY3YE community through its existing protocols. Sri Lanka's government has no vote here, as it appropriately would have no vote in the internal governance of any foreign company operating in the country.

Critically: MY3YE publishes all internal governance decisions to an on-chain record accessible to Layer 2 audit. Transparency is the price of operational autonomy.

**Layer 2 — Joint Governance Council**

A nine-seat council governing the Sovereign Digital Economic Zone:
- 3 seats: Sri Lanka government nominees (Ministry of Digital Economy, CBSL, BOI)
- 3 seats: MY3YE community representatives (elected by DPC weight, not appointed by founders)
- 2 seats: Independent observers (one multilateral — UNDP, IMF, or ADB; one academic)
- 1 seat: Rotating diaspora representative (elected by registered diaspora contributors)

No single party holds a majority. A quorum of five of nine is required for decisions. All decisions are published on-chain within 48 hours of issuance.

Jurisdiction: SDEZ policy, joint fund allocation, milestone review, compliance reporting.

**Layer 3 — Sovereign Oversight**

Sri Lanka's government retains full and unconditional regulatory authority. Parliamentary law supersedes any SDEZ framework. CBSL retains monetary authority. BOI retains investment approval authority. There is no mechanism by which the SDEZ can override Sri Lankan sovereign law — and no request has been made for one.

### 6.3 Constitutional Limits (Immutable)

These terms cannot be changed by either party without dissolving the agreement:

1. **Capital does not govern.** Investment in MY3YE purchases no governance weight in the community. Only verified contribution through the DPC system earns governance participation. This protects the movement from capture — including from political interests.

2. **Sri Lanka retains full sovereignty.** MY3YE operates within Sri Lankan law, within Port City SEZ frameworks, and within whatever agreements BOI and CBSL establish. No extraterritorial claims. No parallel legal system.

3. **All joint funds are on-chain and publicly auditable.** Every dollar of green bond proceeds, FDI flows through BOI, and SDEZ revenue is recorded on a public ledger. This is an IMF transparency advantage, not a liability.

4. **Both parties retain the right of exit.** Sri Lanka may revoke the SDEZ framework with twelve months' notice. MY3YE may withdraw operations with six months' notice. Assets and intellectual property return to their respective owners. There are no hostage dynamics in this agreement.

5. **Contributor protections are paramount.** The 50,000 people who build this system own their earned governance weight, their revenue share, and their ONEON identity credentials. These cannot be revoked if the agreement dissolves. Their participation is theirs.

6. **Founder governance sunset.** MY3YE founders' governance weight decays over four years until it matches earned contribution. There is no permanent governance premium for the founding team.

### 6.4 Dispute Resolution

- Operational disputes within MY3YE: SOS arbitration protocols
- SDEZ policy disputes: Joint Governance Council (five-of-nine quorum)
- Legal disputes: Sri Lanka commercial courts (Port City jurisdiction)
- Constitutional violations: Independent tribunal — one Sri Lanka appointee, one MY3YE community appointee, one multilateral observer

## Part VII: Comparable Precedents

### 7.1 El Salvador — What Not to Do

El Salvador adopted Bitcoin as legal tender in 2021. By 2023, 92% of transactions in the country were not using Bitcoin. By January 2025, under IMF pressure, El Salvador reversed the legal tender status and the government stopped accepting Bitcoin for tax payments.

The lesson is not that a small nation cannot leverage blockchain technology for economic transformation. The lesson is that mandatory adoption — crypto as legal tender, without the institutional infrastructure, workforce readiness, or voluntary market participation that drives real usage — fails. The mandate ran ahead of the ecosystem.

MY3YE's proposal is structurally different. No legal tender. No mandatory adoption. Voluntary participation, confined to Port City SEZ jurisdiction, building on a population that has already self-organized around cryptocurrency at 4.97% penetration. The ecosystem exists. We are building the institutional layer above it — not the other way around.

### 7.2 Bhutan — Covert Sovereignty

Bhutan did not announce a national Bitcoin strategy. It began mining Bitcoin quietly in 2019 using its abundant hydroelectric surplus. By 2024, without a single press release, Bhutan had accumulated approximately $374 million in Bitcoin reserves — equivalent to roughly 27% of its GDP.

The lesson: sovereign asset accumulation through productive means (mining from renewable energy surplus) is IMF-compatible, requires no public declaration, and compounds quietly. Sri Lanka has renewable energy capacity — particularly in the north and east — that is underutilized. A BTC mining program, assessed carefully, could apply the Bhutan model without the risks of the El Salvador model.

This is not a core ask of the Phase 1 proposal. It is a Phase 3 option contingent on the CBSL sandbox success and government comfort. We raise it because the precedent is real, documented, and instructive.

### 7.3 Estonia — Governance as Competitive Advantage

Estonia built a national digital identity infrastructure (e-Residency) that has attracted more than 100,000 digital residents, generated significant FDI, and positioned the country as a technology governance model globally. The e-Residency program is not primarily a revenue program — it is a positioning program that made Estonia the reference implementation for digital governance.

Sri Lanka's population is 22 million. Estonia's is 1.3 million. Sri Lanka's crypto adoption is already higher, per capita, than any Baltic state. The diaspora network — 3 million Sri Lankans abroad, with significant concentrations in technology-intensive economies (UK, Australia, Canada, UAE, United States) — is larger and more globally distributed than Estonia's.

ONEON, deployed in partnership with the Sri Lankan government through SL-UDI, could position Sri Lanka to do what Estonia did — and at a scale Estonia could never achieve.

### 7.4 Panama and Portugal — Regulatory Clarity as Magnet

Both Panama and Portugal established clear, welcoming regulatory frameworks for digital asset businesses without adopting crypto as legal tender. Both attracted significant FDI and talent relocation from jurisdictions with regulatory uncertainty.

Port City Colombo is already positioned as Sri Lanka's regulatory innovation zone. Two crypto licenses have already been issued. The opportunity to be Southeast Asia's Dubai — a recognized hub of legitimate digital economy activity — is accessible without any change to Sri Lanka's national monetary policy.
