# F&F Investment Document — Research Brief
*Compiled 2026-03-29 for the MY3YE / Ottolabs F&F round*

---

## Purpose of This Document Type

A Family & Friends (F&F) investment document differs fundamentally from a VC deck:

- **Audience**: Non-sophisticated investors who trust you personally, not your metrics
- **Tone**: Direct, honest, relationship-preserving — not salesy
- **Legal function**: Creates a paper trail that protects the founder AND the investor
- **Emotional function**: Gives investors a way to say yes (or no) without social awkwardness

---

## Structural Outline

### Section 1 — Plain Language Summary (Executive Overview)
**What it must accomplish**: Let a non-technical person understand what they're putting money into in under 2 minutes.

Include:
- What the company does (one sentence)
- Why you're raising this way (personal trust, early stage, speed)
- The amount you're raising and what you're offering (equity %, SAFE, convertible note)
- The minimum investment threshold
- What happens next (timeline, how they get paid back or equity converted)

**Key principle**: No jargon. If your grandfather can't understand it, rewrite it.

---

### Section 2 — The Opportunity (Why This, Why Now)
**What it must accomplish**: Give investors something to believe in — not just numbers, but a narrative they can hold.

Include:
- The problem being solved (make it human, not market-size)
- Why you are the person to solve it (personal credibility, track record, obsession)
- Why now (timing signal — not vague optimism, but something specific and observable)
- The "closing window" — what changes if you don't act in the next 12–18 months

**Key principle**: F&F investors bet on the person first. Lead with your story, not the TAM.

---

### Section 3 — The Business Model (How You Make Money)
**What it must accomplish**: Show that a path to revenue exists — not a complex financial model, but a believable mechanism.

Include:
- Who pays, for what, how much (simple unit economics)
- Current status: pre-revenue / first customers / growing
- Revenue milestones you expect in 12, 24, 36 months
- How this investment accelerates those milestones

**Key principle**: Be conservative. F&F investors remember what you said and will bring it up at family dinner if projections miss.

---

### Section 4 — Use of Funds
**What it must accomplish**: Show exactly where their money goes. Vagueness destroys trust.

Include:
- Itemized budget breakdown (development, operations, marketing, runway)
- Total raise target and how much is committed vs. open
- How long the runway extends with this capital
- What specific milestones this unlocks

**Key principle**: Precision signals respect. "We'll use it to grow the team" is not acceptable — name the roles, the costs, the timeline.

---

### Section 5 — The Terms (Investment Structure)
**What it must accomplish**: Explain the legal instrument in plain language so investors understand what they own.

**Recommended instrument**: SAFE (Simple Agreement for Future Equity) with a valuation cap and optional discount. Simpler than a convertible note, no interest accrual, no maturity date pressure.

Include:
- Valuation cap (e.g., $3M — this is the max valuation at which their investment converts to equity)
- Discount rate (e.g., 20% — they get equity at a lower price than the next round)
- What triggers conversion (qualified financing round, acquisition, IPO, dissolution)
- Liquidation preference (what happens if the company is sold for less than the cap)
- No guaranteed return — be explicit. This is equity, not a loan.

**Legal protection for founder**:
- Use a standard SAFE template (YC SAFE or equivalent)
- Have a securities attorney review before distributing (even for F&F rounds, securities law applies)
- Issue proper disclosure about risks
- Ensure investors sign and date — verbal agreements are not enforceable

---

### Section 6 — Risks (The Honest Section)
**What it must accomplish**: Demonstrate integrity. Protect yourself legally. Help investors make an informed decision.

Include (explicitly):
- Most startups fail — this investment could go to zero
- Liquidity risk: there is no public market for shares; money may be locked up for 5–10 years
- Dilution: future fundraising will dilute their ownership
- Execution risk: the plan depends on assumptions that may be wrong
- Founder risk: key-person dependency on you

**Why this matters**: If a deal goes badly and you didn't disclose risks, the relationship damage is catastrophic AND you may have legal exposure for fraudulent misrepresentation.

---

### Section 7 — The Ask (How to Invest)
**What it must accomplish**: Make it easy to say yes. Remove friction from the mechanics.

Include:
- Minimum and maximum investment per person
- Deadline to commit
- Exactly how to transfer funds (wire instructions, Wise, etc.)
- What documentation they'll receive and when
- Your direct contact information for questions

---

### Section 8 — About the Team
**What it must accomplish**: Give investors confidence that the people they trust are the right people to execute.

Include:
- Founder bio (relevant experience, why this problem)
- Key team members if any
- Advisors if credible
- What you've already built / shipped (evidence of execution, not just vision)

---

## Relationship Protection Principles

### What kills F&F relationships when deals go wrong:
1. **Surprise**: Investors who didn't understand the risk feel betrayed when they lose money — even if you told them. Over-communicate risk.
2. **Silence**: Not updating investors on bad news is worse than the bad news itself. Commit to quarterly updates.
3. **Informal promises**: Never say "you'll get your money back in 2 years" — only put what you can legally back in writing.
4. **Pressure**: If someone hesitates, give them an easy out. The relationship is worth more than the capital.
5. **Missing the minimum documentation**: A signed term sheet or SAFE protects both parties — skipping paperwork for convenience creates legal and relational landmines.

### What builds lasting trust:
- Telling them bad news before good news
- Sending regular updates (even one paragraph every quarter)
- Treating small investors with the same respect as large ones
- Being clear about what "success" looks like and how they'll know if you're getting there

---

## ROI / Timeline Framing

### For non-sophisticated investors, frame return expectations as:
1. **Base case (likely)**: Capital returned at 1–3x over 5–7 years if company reaches modest revenue
2. **Upside case (possible)**: 5–10x if company achieves significant scale or is acquired
3. **Downside case (real)**: Total loss. This is startup equity, not a savings account.

### What to avoid:
- Specific ROI percentages (implies a guaranteed return — legally problematic)
- Short timelines ("2 years") unless backed by a clear mechanism (e.g., revenue-based repayment)
- Comparison to stock market returns (different risk profile entirely)

---

## Legal Minimum Checklist

- [ ] SAFE or convertible note prepared by attorney (not DIY)
- [ ] Risk disclosure section included and investor acknowledges reading it
- [ ] Investor qualification check (depending on jurisdiction — some require accredited investor status)
- [ ] Signed agreement returned before funds accepted
- [ ] Cap table updated with all investors
- [ ] Quarterly update commitment in writing
- [ ] No verbal side agreements (put everything in writing)
