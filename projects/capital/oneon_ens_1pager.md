## The Problem

ENS solved a hard problem: human-readable names that resolve to cryptographic addresses. But resolution is where the story ends. A .eth name today tells you *where* to reach someone on-chain. It tells you nothing about *who they are*, provides no channel to actually communicate with them, and disappears the moment a platform goes offline.

The gap between "having an ENS name" and "having a functioning sovereign identity and communications presence" is where billions of people fall through. Platforms still own the layer between the name and the voice.

---

## ONEON: The Layer ENS Was Always Building Toward

ONEON is a sovereign layered identity and communications protocol, built to extend ENS names from address resolution into a full identity stack.

**ENS integration is structural, not cosmetic:**

- **ENS as the naming anchor** — Every ONEON identity is rooted in an ENS name. Your .eth name becomes the cryptographic seed for your ONEON profile, portable across every application that reads the standard.
- **Extended text records** — ONEON defines an open schema for ENS text record fields: mesh node endpoint, communications public key, contribution attestations, and ecosystem-specific reputation anchors. Any ENS-compatible application gains the ability to resolve not just an address, but a full identity presence.
- **CCIP-Read resolver (EIP-3668)** — ONEON data lives off-chain by design (scalable, private-by-default). An open CCIP-Read resolver lets any ENS lookup silently extend into the ONEON identity layer — zero-change integration for existing ENS tooling.
- **Subname delegation** — Community namespaces (e.g., `community.oneon.eth`) enable governed subname registries for organizations, DAOs, and ecosystems — each subname a full ONEON identity, governed by ENS L2 infrastructure.

The result: every ENS name becomes the entry point to a censorship-resistant, mesh-networked communications layer. The name is already there. ONEON connects it to a voice that cannot be silenced.
